# Reflective RAG: Self-Evaluation Driven Strategy Optimization in Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation 

Haiyan Wu, Chenchen Wang, Chaoqun Sun, Chengxiong Lu, Yanhong Chen , Zhiqiang Zhang *, Xiaoqing Feng 

School of Information Technology and Artificial Intelligence, Zhejiang University of Finance and Economics, China 

> {wuhy2020,wccc,sunchaoqun,luchengxiong,cyhong,zqzhang,fenglinda}@zufe.edu.cn

Abstract 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a widely adopted paradigm for grounding Large Language Models (LLMs) in external knowledge. Recent agentic RAG sys-tems introduce multi-turn reasoning, but they often lack the capacity to evaluate the utility of retrieved information, leading to brittle rea-soning and suboptimal decision-making. We propose Reflective RAG, an agentic framework that incorporates self-evaluation to dynamically optimize retrieval and generation strategy. At its core, Reflective RAG employs a reflection tagging mechanism that allows the model to cri-tique the relevance of retrieved content, thereby explicitly guiding its subsequent policy. To en-sure robust learning, we introduce a two-stage training procedure that partially decouples eval-uation semantics from strategy optimization. First, during supervised fine-tuning (SFT), the model learns to generate accurate reflection sig-nals by self-correcting labels based on internal uncertainty. Second, a reinforcement learning (RL) stage optimizes the agent’s strategy us-ing these reflections, stabilized by dynamic KL regularization. Evaluations across five knowledge-intensive QA benchmarks demon-strate that Reflective RAG consistently outper-forms strong agentic baselines. Further analy-sis demonstrates its improved training stability and stronger generalization to complex multi-hop reasoning tasks.

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional proficiency in language understand-ing and generation across various natural language processing tasks (Zhao et al., 2023; Naveed et al., 2025). However, their reliance on static parametric knowledge limits their performance in knowledge-intensive domains, leading to information gaps or hallucinations when dealing with rare facts (Kand-pal et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) addresses these limitations by integrating external informa-tion with parametric knowledge, enhancing fac-tual grounding and enabling more comprehensive, timely, and reliable outputs (Lewis et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024). However, vanilla RAG methods typically rely on a static “retrieve-then-generate” paradigm, per-forming retrieval based solely on the initial in-put. This lack of adaptive retrieval during genera-tion severely limits their effectiveness in complex, multi-hop reasoning scenarios. Without the flexi-bility to dynamically determine when and what to retrieve, these methods struggle with information integration in multi-hop tasks. Recent studies have introduced interactive strategies within the reason-ing process, including query decomposition (Press et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), self-correction mech-anisms (Asai et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024), and active retrieval (Jiang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025). Furthermore, with the rise of reinforcement learn-ing in LLM training (Zhang et al., 2025b), agen-

13252 tic RAG has demonstrated improved reasoning by utilizing rule-based rewards derived from final out-puts (Song et al., 2025; Jin et al., 2025). Although existing methods introduce reflection signals or rely on outcome-based optimization, they typically do not explicitly model evaluation as a structured and trainable component that directly constrains policy optimization during multi-turn reasoning, as illus-trated in Figure 1. Therefore, assessment is often delegated to external expert models or decoupled from policy learning, limiting the model’s ability to identify redundant or misleading information and adapt its strategies in multi-turn reasoning. This disconnect can lead to premature commitment to final answers under high uncertainty, undermining the reliability of multi-turn retrieval reasoning. To address these limitations, we present Reflec-tive RAG , a general framework designed to assess retrieved content utility through reflection tagging and refine multi-turn retrieval and generation via explicit evaluation-guided strategy constraints. Re-flective RAG is fully self-contained, alleviating dependence on external expert models. We intro-duce a bifurcated training process to ensure ro-bust policy learning: (1) the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage, with the model self-correcting eval-uation labels via internal likelihood signals and learning basic post-evaluation strategies; and (2) the reinforcement learning (RL) stage, applying evaluation-guided strategy constraints to regulate rollout trajectories and dynamic KL regularization to stabilize both evaluation semantics and policy updates. This decoupling ensures that the model develops interpretable assessment signals that offer direct guidance for reasoning. The contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose Reflective RAG, an agentic RAG framework that incorporates explicit self-evaluation into multi-turn retrieval reasoning, and constructs high-quality training trajecto-ries via self-corrected evaluation signals with-out external supervision (Section 3.2). • We introduce an evaluation-aware optimiza-tion strategy that combines evaluation-guided strategy constraints with decaying KL regu-larization on evaluation labels (Section 3.3), enabling stable semantics and effective policy optimization in multi-turn reasoning. • Extensive experiments (Table 1) on five knowledge-intensive QA benchmarks show that Reflective RAG consistently outperforms strong baselines, and ablations as well as quan-titative analyses further confirm its advantages in training stability and generalization. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation 

Retrieval-augmented generation enhances LLMs by incorporating external knowledge to improve factual grounding, completeness, and reliabil-ity (Lewis et al., 2020; Arslan et al., 2024). Prior work in RAG has primarily pursued two comple-mentary directions: enhancing the effectiveness of retrievers and improving the capacity of LLMs to utilize retrieved knowledge effectively. Retriever-oriented approaches aim to improve retrieval rele-vance and alignment with LLM preferences (Glass et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025b), while LLM-centric methods explore task-adaptive fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024), self-reflection (Asai et al., 2024), and preference modeling to handle noisy or misleading informa-tion (Xu et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024). These meth-ods enhance RAG by aligning retrieval with genera-tion and filtering irrelevant content. However, most do not systematically model retrieval interactions in multi-hop reasoning and rely on expert-labeled or manually annotated trajectories. In contrast, Reflec-tive RAG enables the model to self-correct evalua-tion labels and enforce strategy constraints, thereby adaptively optimizing reasoning strategies without external expert supervision. 

2.2 Agentic RAG with Reinforcement Learning 

Recently, with advances in agentic RAG (Singh et al., 2025), approaches have increasingly inte-grated retrieval with multi-hop reasoning, trans-forming retrieval from a static process into a dy-namic one that can adaptively decide when and what to retrieve during reasoning. For example, ReAct (Yao et al., 2022) introduces a collabora-tive paradigm. SearChain (Xu et al., 2024) con-ducts multi-round retrieval by progressively decom-posing questions, and Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025) incorporates retrieval and information integration directly into the reasoning process. To further enhance models’ ability to interact with retrieval tools, inspired by DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), an increasing number of agentic RAG methods adopt reinforcement learning for training (Liang 13253 et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a). For instance, ReSearch (Chen et al., 2025), R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025) and Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) rely solely on trajectory format and final answer correct-ness as reward signals. CARE (Wang et al., 2025) combines retrieval-aware rewards with curriculum learning. MT-PPO (Wei et al., 2025) extends trajectory-level rewards to turn-level optimization objectives. StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025) pro-poses a token-level stepwise reward mechanism based on information gain and redundancy penal-ties. However, these methods generally lack ex-plicit and trainable evaluation mechanisms of inter-mediate retrieval results and corresponding strategy constraints. In contrast, Reflective RAG explicitly models self-evaluation across multi-turn retrieval interactions and incorporates these evaluations as strategy constraints during rollout sampling, en-abling stable optimization of evaluation-aware poli-cies for multi-turn retrieval reasoning.

Evaluation-aware Trajectory Reconstruction. 

After self-correcting the evaluation labels, we per-form constrained resampling of subsequent rea-soning steps to construct high-quality trajecto-ries that align evaluation with model strategy. Specifically, for each retrieval step, the model adapts its strategy based on the corrected la-13255 bel: Useful—fully leverage the retrieved informa-tion; Redundant—attenuate reliance on it; Con-fusing—ignore it and trigger new reasoning or re-trieval. The exact prompt templates used to imple-ment these strategies are provided in Appendix D.2. Leveraging corrected evaluation labels, we iter-atively resample retrieval and reasoning steps to generate trajectories that align with these labels. This refined dataset is then employed for Super-vised Fine-Tuning (SFT), effectively bridging the gap between evaluation metrics and the original generation strategy. 

3.3 Reinforcement Learning Phase 

After acquiring initial self-evaluation capabilities and a basic evaluation-guided strategy through SFT, we further optimize the model using reinforcement learning to enhance stability and policy consistency in multi-turn retrieval reasoning. We adopt Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024), which maximizes trajectory-level rewards while constraining semantic drift in evaluation la-bels and stabilizing the model’s post-evaluation pol-icy during rollout through evaluation-guided strat-egy constraints. The detailed GRPO objective and optimization procedure with our modifications are provided in Appendix B. 

Evaluation-Guided Strategy Constraints. Dur-ing rollout, we apply selective, evaluation-guided strategy constraints. Specifically, if a retrieval is labeled “Confusing” but the model attempts to di-rectly generate the final answer, we enforce at least one additional retrieval or reasoning step to prevent premature termination under potentially mislead-ing information. This constraint is also applied at inference to ensure more reliable reasoning. In contrast, for “Useful” and “Redundant” labels, no hard constraints are imposed, allowing the model to continue retrieval or generate an answer freely. This design minimizes intervention while preserv-ing exploration flexibility.

Reward Design. Unlike most RL-based agen-tic RAG methods that rely on complex or exter-nally engineered rewards, we adopt a sparse yet stable trajectory-level reward that focuses solely on whether the model produces a complete reasoning trajectory with a correct final answer and a valid output format:

4.2 Baselines 

We evaluate Reflective RAG against a compre-hensive suite of baselines representing different paradigms of reasoning and retrieval: 1) CoT (Wei et al., 2022): A reasoning-only baseline that per-forms multi-hop inference without external knowl-edge retrieval. 2) Vanilla RAG (Ram et al., 2023): The standard “retrieve-then-read” paradigm, where the generator produces an answer based on a sin-gle initial retrieval step. 3) Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024): An adaptive framework that utilizes reflec-tion tokens for retrieval and refinement via Super-vised Fine-Tuning (SFT), but notably lacks a re-inforcement learning (RL) component for policy adaptation. 4) Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025): A multi-turn retrieval baseline designed for information in-tegration that does not incorporate RL optimization. 5) s3 (Jiang et al., 2025a): A lightweight RL frame-work that decouples search from generation and learns data-efficient retrieval policies via a Gain-Beyond-RAG reward. 6) Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025): A recent agentic RAG approach that em-ploys multi-turn retrieval and is trained via RL, using final answer correctness as the reward signal. Furthermore, to ensure a fair comparison, we evaluate all baselines with Qwen2.5 as the back-bone, except for Self-RAG, where we use the LLaMA2-7B checkpoint released by the authors.

4.4 Ablation Study 

To assess the individual contributions of each train-ing component in Reflective RAG, we conducted a comprehensive ablation study across all datasets using the Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct backbone. The re-sults are summarized in Table 2. 1) Limitations of SFT . While Supervised Fine-Tuning alone (SFT Only) yields noticeable improvements over the base model (Original), demonstrating its ability to enhance task adaptability, it still underperforms reinforcement learning-based training. Specifically, although SFT successfully instills basic competen-cies such as evaluation label prediction and out-put formatting, it proves insufficient for mastering the complex dynamics of multi-turn retrieval and information integration required for robust trajec-tory generation. 2) Criticality of Constraints and Regularization . When SFT is trained without eval-uation label correction and trajectory reconstruc-tion (No correction), the resulting model produces noisier evaluation signals, which in turn weak-ens the effectiveness of both strategy constraints and evaluation-aware KL regularization during RL. Moreover, removing strategy constraints during RL training (No Cons.) leads to a significant perfor-mance decline across most benchmarks, indicating that constraining the generation policy is essen-tial for stabilizing multi-hop retrieval processes guided by self-evaluation. Furthermore, eliminat-ing KL regularization on evaluation labels (No eval. KL) degrades performance. These findings confirm that label correction and trajectory reconstruction during SFT, together with strategy constraints and evaluation-aware KL regularization in RL, are criti-cal for training stability. They ensure that the model accurately learns evaluation signals and generation strategies, ultimately producing more reliable rea-soning trajectories.

5 Conclusion 

In this work, we present Reflective RAG, an evaluation-aware agentic retrieval-augmented framework that enhances multi-turn reasoning via self-evaluation signals. Using self-corrected evalu-ation labels, incorporating evaluation-guided strat-egy constraints, and applying KL regularization on these labels, the framework improves reasoning sta-bility and enables judicious use of retrieved infor-mation. Experiments on five knowledge-intensive QA benchmarks show that Reflective RAG consis-tently outperforms strong baselines, with ablations confirming the effectiveness of each component in stabilizing reasoning and improving generaliza-tion. This work advances more robust and con-sistent agentic RAG systems guided by internal self-evaluation mechanisms. 

Limitations 

Despite its effectiveness, Reflective RAG has sev-eral limitations that suggest directions for future research. 

Training and evaluation on larger models. Our experiments primarily focus on small to medium-sized LLMs, which exhibit higher reasoning un-certainty, making deficiencies in retrieval utiliza-tion and reasoning stability more apparent. Reflec-tive RAG shows clear improvements in this setting. However, systematic training and evaluation on larger models are necessary to verify the frame-work’s generalizability and effectiveness at higher model capacities. 

Evaluation signals are not directly optimized as rewards. In Reflective RAG, self-evaluation sig-nals are used to impose strategy constraints rather than as direct rewards. Preliminary attempts to design turn-level rewards based on evaluation la-bels led to reward hacking, where the model over-predicted “Useful” while avoiding “Redundant” and “Confusing”, thereby distorting the semantics of evaluation signals. Designing reward mecha-nisms that leverage self-evaluation without induc-ing such degenerate behaviors remains an open challenge. 

Coarse-grained evaluation categories. The cur-rent reflection tagging mechanism classifies re-trieved content into coarse-grained utility cate-gories (e.g., Useful, Redundant, Confusing). While sufficient for high-level strategy constraints, these labels may overlook finer distinctions in evidence quality, relevance, or reliability. This limitation be-comes particularly relevant in settings where mul-tiple documents are retrieved at a single step, as different documents may vary in correctness or usefulness while being assigned a shared evalu-ation signal. Incorporating more fine-grained or structured evaluation signals could enable more nuanced strategy modulation, particularly in com-plex multi-hop reasoning scenarios or ambiguous retrieval tasks.

A Percentile-based Evaluation Label Assignment 

For each retrieval step, we assign an evaluation label based on the percentile distribution of its per-plexity difference ∆PPL . Specifically, steps with 

∆PPL > 0 that fall within the top λe% of the pos-itive ∆PPL distribution are labeled as “Useful”. Steps whose ∆PPL lies in the bottom λe/2% of the positive ∆PPL distribution or in the top λe/2% 

of the negative ∆PPL distribution are labeled as “Redundant”. Finally, steps with ∆PPL < 0 that fall within the bottom λe% of the negative ∆PPL 

distribution are labeled as “Confusing”. This percentile-based labeling strategy relies solely on the model’s internal generation proba-bilities and requires neither external expert models nor manual annotations, thereby enabling fully self-supervised correction of evaluation labels.

D More Details on Experiment Setup 

D.1 Implementation Details 

We conduct supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning (RL) using the LLaMA-Factory (Zheng et al., 2024) and verl (Sheng et al., 2025) frameworks, respectively. All experiments are performed on a single node with four 80GB GPUs. For SFT, the model is trained on a cu-rated dataset of 22k trajectories, combining fil-tered samples from NQ, HotpotQA, and Trivi-aQA (Joshi et al., 2017). We use AdamW opti-mizer (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017) with an initial learning rate of 2e-6, a warmup ratio of 0.1, and a total batch size of 256 for one epoch to avoid overfitting. For RL, NQ and HotpotQA are merged into a unified training set. Training uses a total batch size of 256, mini-batch size of 128, micro-batch size of 20, and a maximum sequence length of 4096. The learning rate is 5e-7 with a warmup ratio of 0.285, trained for Ttrain = 300 steps with a clip ratio ϵ = 0 .2. During rollout, vLLM is employed to sample five response trajectories per prompt. Checkpoints are saved every 60 steps, and the model used for evaluation is either the final checkpoint or the last checkpoint before any re-ward collapse.
